US vs UK Nutrition Label Analysis

By Akash Sriram

US Nutrition Label Overview

The US nutrition label is meticulously designed for clarity and visual hierarchy, adhering to FDA guidelines with a structured typographic approach. The title "Nutrition Facts" is rendered in Helvetica Regular with an 8-point leading, featuring "Nutrition" in a larger size and "Facts" in smaller, italicized text for a balanced look. Serving size and calorie information are delineated by 3-point and 7-point rules, respectively, directing the reader’s eye effectively. Percent Daily Values are set in Franklin Gothic Heavy at a minimum of 12 points, framed by a 1/2-point rule, while Helvetica Black highlights key data, ensuring a precise and readable layout that enhances consumer understanding.

The serving size on the US label is prominently displayed in a bold, large font, such as "1/3 cup (55g)" for 8 servings per container, reflecting updated standards to help consumers easily identify a single portion and its nutritional impact.
Calories are a focal point, presented in an oversized, bold font like "240" under "Amount per serving," separated by a bold line, enabling quick recognition for dietary planning.
Updated Daily Values (% DV) align with current science, displayed in a clear two-column format (e.g., total fat 10%, sodium 7%), with a footnote tying them to a 2,000-calorie diet for straightforward nutrient assessment.
The label now requires reporting of added sugars, vitamin D, and potassium, showing "Total Sugars 12g, including 10g Added Sugars (20% DV)" alongside vitamin D (2mcg, 10% DV) and potassium (240mg, 6% DV) for a comprehensive health overview.
Essential nutrients like vitamin D, calcium, iron, and potassium are mandated, with values such as iron (8mg, 45% DV) and vitamin A (2mcg, 20% DV) listed, providing a detailed nutritional profile.

UK Nutrition Label Overview

The UK nutrition label prioritizes accessibility and consumer choice with a flexible, functional design, diverging from the US’s rigid typographic standards. Unlike the US, which specifies fonts like Helvetica and Franklin Gothic with precise sizes (e.g., 8-point leading), the UK follows EU Regulation 1169/2011, mandating only a minimum font size for key data while allowing font variety for readability. A standout feature is the voluntary traffic light system, using red, amber, and green indicators for fat, saturated fat, sugars, and salt on the front of pack, offering a visual shortcut absent in the US’s text-heavy, calorie-focused design.

The UK label provides serving size data per 100g or 100ml as the primary reference (e.g., "200kJ / 48kcal per 100g"), with per-portion details optional, enabling uniform product comparisons unlike the US’s bold, serving-specific approach.
Energy is displayed in both kilojoules and kilocalories per 100g (e.g., "1979kJ / 473kcal"), offering a dual-unit system for international alignment, contrasting with the US’s singular, prominent calorie focus.
The UK requires energy, fat, saturated fat, carbohydrates, sugars, protein, and salt per 100g (e.g., fat 17.4g, salt 0.9g), omitting % Daily Values unless voluntary, favoring raw data over the US’s interpreted % DV format.
The voluntary traffic light system highlights added sugars and fats with color codes (e.g., red for high sugars at 34g), differing from the US’s text-based "10g Added Sugars (20% DV)" approach.
The traffic light system rates salt (e.g., 0.9g, 15% RI, amber) visually, providing a quick health cue, unlike the US’s detailed nutrient list without color coding.
